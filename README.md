**BRIEF OF AMICUS CURIAE [NAME] IN SUPPORT OF PETITIONERS**

## Interest of Amicus Curiae

Amicus submits this brief because this case concerns the proper allocation of authority among the political branches, the constitutional limits of federal judicial power, and the rights of millions of federal student-loan borrowers affected by a national emergency. This case also presents a threshold question of exceptional importance: whether Article III standing can be manufactured through an asserted injury to a legally distinct loan-servicing entity that did not itself sue. The Court’s answer to that question reaches far beyond student-loan policy and threatens to expand judicial power at the expense of the Constitution’s case-or-controversy requirement. In Amicus’s view, the decision below departed from ordinary standing principles and improperly displaced authority that Congress had delegated to the Executive Branch through the HEROES Act. The HEROES Act authorizes the Secretary of Education to “waive or modify” statutory and regulatory provisions applicable to federal student financial assistance programs as necessary to ensure that affected borrowers “are not placed in a worse position financially” because of a national emergency. ([Legal Information Institute][1])

## Summary of Argument

The Court should not have reached the merits because the plaintiff States lacked Article III standing. The majority’s standing theory rested on alleged financial harm to MOHELA, a separate legal entity that did not bring this suit. The dissent correctly explained that the Court distorted standing doctrine in order to create a justiciable controversy where none existed “by any ordinary measure.” The Court thus allowed one entity to litigate based on the purported injury of another, even though MOHELA has its own legal personality and the power to sue and be sued in its own name. ([Supreme Court][2])

Even if the Court had properly reached the merits, the better reading of the HEROES Act favored the Secretary. Congress used broad language. It empowered the Secretary to “waive or modify any statutory or regulatory provision” applicable to federal student financial assistance programs when necessary in connection with war, military operation, or national emergency. The dissent concluded that the statute, “read as written,” gave the Secretary broad authority to relieve the effects of a national emergency on borrowers’ ability to repay their loans. ([Legal Information Institute][1])

The majority’s contrary approach substituted a narrowing gloss for the text Congress enacted. It also relied on an expansive version of the so-called major questions doctrine to invalidate an exercise of delegated authority that fell within the statute’s ordinary language. As Justice Kagan wrote, the majority’s “normal” statutory interpretation could not sustain its result, and the Court instead used a special rule to defeat significant regulatory action. ([Supreme Court][2])

## Argument

### I. The Court lacked Article III jurisdiction because the plaintiff States failed to establish standing.

Article III requires a plaintiff to show a concrete and particularized injury fairly traceable to the challenged action and likely to be redressed by judicial relief. In this case, the State of Missouri was permitted to proceed on the theory that debt cancellation would reduce servicing revenue for MOHELA. But MOHELA did not sue. Missouri instead attempted to assert standing through the alleged injury of a separate public corporation. The majority acknowledged the argument that MOHELA has a legal personality separate from the State, yet treated that separateness as no barrier to Missouri’s suit. ([Supreme Court][2])

That move was unsound. A distinct corporate entity is ordinarily required to vindicate its own legal interests. The government’s merits briefing in the case likewise described MOHELA as a “state-created nonprofit corporation” and argued that Missouri could not piggyback on MOHELA’s alleged injury to manufacture standing.  The dissent was more direct: the majority “distort[ed] standing doctrine to create a case fit for judicial resolution,” although there was “no such case here, by any ordinary measure.” ([Supreme Court][2])

Subsequent litigation has reinforced the weakness of treating MOHELA and Missouri as interchangeable. In 2024, the Tenth Circuit held that MOHELA had **not** carried its burden to show that it is an arm of the State of Missouri for Eleventh Amendment purposes, and expressly stated that *Biden v. Nebraska* did not resolve that issue. Although that later decision does not alter the historical record of this case, it underscores the fragility of the premise that harm to MOHELA should automatically count as harm to Missouri itself. 

Where standing is doubtful, federal courts must stop. They may not reach out to decide highly consequential policy disputes merely because the subject matter is important. The constitutional role of the judiciary is limited to actual cases and controversies, not generalized disagreements over federal policy. Here, the Court exceeded that limit.

### II. The HEROES Act granted broad authority to waive or modify student-loan obligations during a national emergency.

Congress spoke in broad terms. The HEROES Act authorizes the Secretary of Education to “waive or modify any statutory or regulatory provision” applicable to federal student financial assistance programs as deemed necessary in connection with specified emergencies. The Act further directs the Secretary to ensure that affected individuals are not placed in a worse position financially because of that emergency. ([Legal Information Institute][1])

That text is expansive, not cramped. “Waive or modify” is not language of trivial adjustment alone. It reflects congressional recognition that national emergencies can require substantial administrative flexibility. Congress did not say the Secretary may make only minor accounting changes. Nor did it confine relief to purely procedural accommodations. It delegated broad remedial authority to protect affected borrowers in extraordinary circumstances. ([Legal Information Institute][1])

The dissent’s reading followed the statute as enacted. Justice Kagan wrote that the statute, “read as written,” gives the Secretary broad authority to relieve a national emergency’s effects on borrowers’ ability to repay student loans, and that the Secretary “did no more than use that lawfully delegated authority.” ([Supreme Court][2]) That interpretation better respects both Congress’s words and Congress’s institutional choice to vest emergency-response discretion in the Executive Branch.

### III. The majority improperly used atextual reasoning to narrow a statute whose text already answered the question.

The majority did not simply read the words Congress enacted and apply them. Instead, it treated the scale of the policy as a reason to distrust the delegation itself. That approach inverted ordinary statutory interpretation. A law does not become narrower because the problem it addresses is large. Nor does delegated authority disappear because its exercise has significant economic and political consequences. The dissent sharply criticized this move, explaining that the majority’s “normal” statutory interpretation could not sustain its decision and that the Court instead invoked the major questions doctrine to defeat the Secretary’s plan. ([Supreme Court][2])

That concern is especially powerful here because Congress had already spoken directly to emergencies. The HEROES Act was enacted precisely to allow the Secretary to respond when borrowers face exceptional disruption caused by war or national emergency. A court should hesitate before converting a broad emergency delegation into a dead letter. Otherwise, the judiciary effectively rewrites a statute under the guise of interpreting it. ([Legal Information Institute][1])

### IV. The Court’s approach undermined democratic accountability rather than protecting it.

The majority framed the issue as one of institutional authority. But the more democratically accountable position was to respect Congress’s choice to delegate emergency powers to the Secretary. Congress enacted the HEROES Act. The Executive implemented that statute during an acknowledged national emergency. If the political branches had exceeded acceptable policy bounds, Congress retained tools to amend the statute, restrict appropriations, or otherwise legislate. The judiciary’s role was not to invent standing and then nullify a broad delegation because the policy was controversial. ([Legal Information Institute][1])

The Constitution’s separation of powers is not preserved when courts assume power they do not possess. It is preserved when each branch remains within its assigned role. Here, that would have meant dismissing for lack of standing or, at minimum, giving the enacted text of the HEROES Act its fair scope.

## Conclusion

The judgment should be reconsidered because the plaintiff States lacked Article III standing and because the HEROES Act authorized the Secretary’s action. The Court’s contrary reasoning elevated speculation over jurisdictional rigor, narrowed broad statutory language without textual warrant, and displaced the policy judgment of the political branches in an area where Congress had expressly delegated authority. For those reasons, the decision was wrong.
